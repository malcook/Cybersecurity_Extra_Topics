Use Google Chrome as your web browser

  • It is fast and state of the art
  • It runs on your phone: Android, iPhone
  • It runs on your computer: Mac, Windows PC, Linux

Best of all:

  • It has very good in-built security and protection features

Use Enhanced Safe Browsing in Google Chrome

  • Google maintains a list of websites which violate Google Safe Browsing Policies
  • Used by all major browsers
  • Chrome will warn you when you start to visit a site on the list
  • But only if you have enabled Enhanced Safe Browsing
  • You can test individual sites yourself using Safe Browsing site status

Demo: Enhanced Safe Browsing Warnings

What to do if a site triggers a Safe Browsing warning

If you get any of these warning you should:

  • STOP
  • Close page
  • Do not override warning

Phishing warning

  • URL: http://testsafebrowsing.appspot.com/s/phishing.html
  • Browser Reaction: Full-page red warning: "Deceptive site ahead."
  • Use Case: Demonstrates how browsers block sites designed to trick users into giving up passwords or credit card info.

Malware warning

  • URL: http://testsafebrowsing.appspot.com/s/malware.html
  • Browser Reaction: Full-page red warning: "The site ahead contains malware."
  • Use Case: Shows the protection against sites that attempt to install viruses or malicious code on a device.

Unwanted software warning

  • URL: http://testsafebrowsing.appspot.com/s/unwanted.html
  • Browser Reaction: Warning: "The site ahead contains harmful programs."
  • Use Case: Explains how browsers flag "adware" or programs that make unexpected changes to your computer (like changing your homepage).

A site with no encryption (HTTP)

  • URL: http://http.badssl.com/
  • Browser Reaction: Shows "Not Secure" label in the address bar.
  • Use Case: Best for showing the basic difference between HTTP and HTTPS.

Recap: "https://", and "Secure" websites.

  • "https://" ONLY means communication is (s)ecurely encrypted between your brower and the website.
    • It does not mean the website is "safe" or "legitimate".

What about the "Padlock" icon?,

Most browsers now

  • trim the leading "https://" from displaying (by default)
  • have replaced the old "padlock" icon with a "shield" icon (Firefox) or "sliders" icon (Chrome).

Why the change

  • removes false sense of security the padlock icon suggested
  • gives more space to the "OmniBox"

It’s definitely a bit of a change for those of us who grew up being told to "look for the lock" before typing a password! The new rule of thumb is: If the browser isn't shouting at you with a "Not Secure" warning, you're on a standard encrypted connection.

Use and protect a Google Account

  • If you don't have one, visit https://google.com, in your web browser, choose sign-in and create one.
  • It will be at the center of your security.
  • Give it an Excellent (Unique, Strong and Memorable) password!!!

Enable "Two-Factor Authentication" (2FA)

What are the different kinds of factors?

  • Knowledge factor – something you know, like a password or PIN. Easiest to hack – there are only so many possible combinations of numbers, letters, and symbols you can use.
  • Possession factor – something you own, like an email or a smartphone. Much harder to hack into an account if you also have a code sent to a phone to confirm that it’s really you.
  • Inherence factor – a part of you (facial ID or fingerprint). Hardest to hack – almost impossible to fake a fingerprint or part of yourself, making this the strongest security option of the three.

Why Not SMS (text messaging)

  • Phone numbers can be hijacked
  • Codes can be intercepted
  • Attackers ask for codes

Configure your Google Chrome "profile"

Perform some basic initial configuration:

  • Add your cell phone as a "recovery phone"
  • Take the options to sync your history and tabs across browsers.

Review your profile's Security settings

  • In Chrome, click on ` three-dots menu > your account > Manage Google Account`
  • select "Security & sign-in"

Install and use Google Authenticator"

For your 2nd factor, prefer use an authenticator app (e.g. Google Authenticator) instead of SMS (text messaging)

  • Codes generated on device
  • Not tied to phone number
  • Works even if phone is offline
  • Protects even if password is stolen

How It Works

  • Scan QR code
  • Enter 6-digit code
  • Rotates every 30 seconds

Backup Codes

  • Write them down
  • Store safely

Critical Rule

  • Never share your codes

Use Google Password Manager

  • You don’t create passwords anymore. The manager does it for you.
  • The are unique, strong. They are not memorable.
  • Google has an is your browser (Chrome or Firefox). The browser does.
  • Let it generate passwords (never reuse passwords)
  • Chrome stores it securely. You don’t need to remember it.

Password Vault

To manage passwords, visit

  • chrome://password-manager/passwords - on your local device
  • https://passwords.google.com/ - in the cloud (if your passwords are sync-ed)

Enable Sync

Synchronize the passwords between your phone and other devices

Once sync is on, everything carries over automatically.

  • Open Settings
  • Go to “You and Google → Sync”
  • Ensure Passwords is ON

Link your Google Account to your Google Chrome "profile"

  • With it, Chrome can synchronize your phone(s) and computer(s): Tabs, Bookmarks, Passwords, Settings, Preferences
  • Use it with sites that support Sign in with Google, such as Reddit, Pinterest, eBay, Uber, DoorDash, etc… (reducing the number of usernames and passwords you need to create or remember!)

Demo: Securing Your Digital Identity on a new site (github)

Demonstrate the "Modern Security Stack":

  • Password: Created and managed by Google Password Manager.
  • Primary 2nd factor: Google Authenticator for high security.
  • Backup 2nd factor: SMS (Text Message) for recovery.

Step 1: Account Creation & Password Generation

  • Go to https://github.com/signup.
  • Use your burner Gmail address.
  • Action: Right-click the Password field and select "Generate password…"
  • Teaching Point: Chrome creates a random string of characters that is impossible to guess, and saves it so you don't have to write it down.

Step 2: Configure SMS 2FA (The "Safety Net")

  • Navigate to Settings (top-right profile icon) > Password and authentication.
  • Under "Two-factor authentication," click "Enable."
  • Choose SMS as the first method.
  • Enter your phone number, receive the text, and type in the code.
  • Teaching Point: Explain that even though we have a phone number linked, we are going to add an even better layer next.

Step 3: Configure Google Authenticator (The "Gold Standard")

  • In the same "Two-factor methods" section, click "Add" next to Authenticator app.
  • A QR code will appear on the projector.
  • Action: Open Google Authenticator on your phone, tap the "+" and scan the code.
  • Type the 6-digit rolling code into GitHub to verify the link.
  • Teaching Point: This is more secure than SMS because the code stays on your device and can't be "intercepted" by scammers.

Step 4: Save Your Recovery Codes in a file

  • GitHub will display a list of one-time use "Recovery Codes".
  • If you lose your phone, you can use each one one-time (only) to get back in.
  • Download it and save it someplace "safe".
  • Confirm that you have downloaded them.

Step 5: Save Your Recovery Codes in Google Password Manage

  • Action: Open Chrome Settings > Passwords and Autofill > Google Password Manager.
  • Find the GitHub entry, click "Edit," and paste these codes into the "Note" section.

Step 6: The "Security Check" Login

  • Log out of GitHub.
  • Click "Sign In" and let Google Password Manager fill the username/password.
  • When prompted for the 2FA code, show the class the 30-second timer on your phone.
  • Enter the code to finish the demo.

Use email "spam filters"

  • Be aware! Automated spam filters make errors (both kinds!).
  • Review the contents of you spam folder periodically
  • Marking messages that you know to be spam helps.

Use AI to test suspicious Emails

  • ChatGPT and Gemini are pretty good spam detectors! You can:
    • Provide a Prompt: “Spam or not? Analyze this email”
    • Paste suspicious email text.
  • Do not include sensitive data!

Keep your phone and computers up-to-date

Configure your phone to automatically perform system updates.

  • Why: Most are for security.
  • How: (example) On Android: choose `System > Search Settings for "System Updates" > Smart Updates: enabled`
