5. Use Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

5.1. what is it

factor of authentication Piece of evidence used to verify your identity during log-in

There are 3 different types of factors:

  • Knowledge factor
  • Possession factor
  • Inherence factor

5.2. What are the different kinds of factors?

Knowledge factor – something you know, like a password or PIN Easiest to hack – there are only so many possible combinations of numbers, letters, and symbols you can use.

Possession factor – something you own, like an email or a smartphone Much harder to hack into an account if you also have a code sent to a phone to confirm that it’s really you.

Inherence factor – a part of you (facial ID or fingerprint) Hardest to hack –almost impossible to fake a fingerprint or part of yourself, making this the strongest security option of the three.

with an Authenticator App

  • Codes generated on device
  • Not tied to phone number
  • Works offline
  • Adds second step
  • Protects even if password is stolen

5.3. Why Not SMS

  • Phone numbers can be hijacked

6. Demo: Password Manager (Live)

6.1. The Problem (Reuse)

  • Example login

Most people reuse passwords. That’s how accounts get taken over. 

Email: demo@example.com
Password: Password123

This is what people actually do. It’s unsafe.

6.2. Let Chrome Generate

  • Right-click password field
  • Select “Suggest strong password”

You don’t create passwords anymore. The browser does.

6.3. Save Password

  • Submit login
  • Click “Save” when prompted

Chrome stores it securely. You don’t need to remember it.

6.4. Autofill Demo

  • Log out
  • Return to login page
  • Select saved account

Next time, it fills it in automatically.

6.5. Password Vault

  • Navigate to:
chrome://password-manager/passwords
  • View saved passwords
  • Open one entry

All your passwords are stored here.

6.6. Enable Sync

  • Open Settings
  • Go to “You and Google → Sync”
  • Ensure Passwords is ON

This makes your passwords available on your phone and other devices.

6.7. Protect the Account

  • Go to:
https://myaccount.google.com/security
  • Enable 2-Step Verification

This only works safely if your Google account is protected. Turn this on. Use an authenticator app, not text messages.

6.8. One Rule

  • Never reuse passwords
  • Let the browser generate them

Never reuse passwords. Let the browser handle it every time.

6.9. Optional: Edit Password

  • Open saved password
  • Replace with generated one

You can always update weak passwords later.

6.10. Optional: Mobile Sync

  • Same passwords appear on phone

Once sync is on, everything carries over automatically.

9. What To Do (Starting Today)

  • Use a password manager. Simplist is your browser (Chrome or Firefox).
  • Let it generate passwords (never reuse passwords)
  • Turn on sync so passwords are available on all devices
  • Protect your main account (e.g. Google mail)
    • give it a very strong password
    • that you will remember
    • use two factor (2FS) authentication for even more security
    • prefer to use an authenticator app (e.g. Google Authenticator) instead of SMS (text messaging) for your 2nd factor
  • Configure your phone to automatically perform system updates. Most are for securit.y
  • Never click links in unexpected messages
  • Never send money or gift cards on request
  • Never share codes or passwords with anyone
  • Verify using a known contact method (not the message)

Keep this blunt and fast. This is the only slide they need to remember. Do not elaborate. Reinforce that partial adoption is still valuable, but these are the targets.
